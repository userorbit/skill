AI Support Triage: An Agent Working Your Inbox, Not Replacing It

The best AI support setup isn't a chatbot deflecting tickets. It's an agent that reads the whole queue, drafts the easy replies, and briefs you on the hard ones.

Most "AI support" is a chatbot standing between users and help, deflecting tickets and hoping. There's a quieter setup that works better: keep the inbox, keep the humans, and put an agent to work inside the queue — reading everything, drafting the routine replies, and packaging context for the ones that need a person.

This is triage, and it's the highest-leverage thing to automate in support, because it's where the time actually goes: figuring out what each thread is, who should handle it, and what the answer probably is.

The morning sweep

With the Userorbit skill, an agent can work the support inbox the way a good support lead does. A single morning prompt:

"Go through the support todo queue. Reply to anything covered by our docs, label and prioritize the rest, and give me a summary with your suggested replies for the tricky ones."

The agent pulls the queue with support-threads.list, reads each timeline and its customer context via support-threads.info, and then splits the work:

  • Known answers — questions covered by the help center get a reply (support-messages.create) and a status flip to done. Because the agent can search your articles, its replies cite real docs instead of hallucinating steps.
  • Bugs — get a label, a priority, and a private note summarizing the repro. Since the agent also has your codebase, it can often attach a first diagnosis — "this matches the pagination change in last week's release" — before an engineer ever looks.
  • Judgment calls — refunds, angry customers, ambiguous requests — get drafted replies left as private notes for a human to review, edit, and send.

The escalation path matters most

Where agent triage usually falls down is the handoff. Two features make it clean:

  • Private notes vs public replies. Everything risky stays internal by default. The agent's rule of thumb: it may send what documentation supports, it must draft everything else. Notes are how it shows its work.
  • Side discussions. When a thread needs engineering, the agent opens a Slack or email side discussion from inside the thread — the crash report goes to #engineering with full context, and the answer comes back attached to the ticket instead of lost in a channel.

Graduating to workflows

After a few weeks of prompted triage, patterns emerge — and patterns are what workflows are for. The rules the agent kept applying by hand ("anything mentioning invoice → Billing label, billing queue") become trigger-driven automations that run on every new thread, no prompt needed. The agent can build these too: describe the rule, and it creates, publishes, and test-runs the workflow.

The division of labor settles into three tiers: workflows handle the mechanical routing, the agent handles reading and drafting, humans handle the sends that carry risk or relationship. Support volume grows; headcount doesn't have to.

What to measure

Three numbers tell you whether it's working: first-response time on the todo queue, the share of threads resolved with a docs-backed reply, and how often humans edit the agent's drafts before sending. The first two should fall and rise fast. The third is your quality dial — when edits get rare, widen what the agent may send on its own.

The setup is the same as every other agent workflow: a Userorbit workspace, an API key, and the skill. The full endpoint surface — queues, messages, labels, assignees, snoozing, merging, snippets — is in the support inbox guide.
